This thesis investigates a bilevel knapsack-scheduling problem where a leader and follower have conflicting objectives regarding system makespan.
In the upper-level problem, the leader selects quantities of job types within a budget constraint to maximize the makespan, thereby maximizing system utilization.
In the lower-level problem, the follower schedules the selected jobs on parallel identical machines to minimize the makespan.
We develop a branch-and-bound algorithm that exploits the hierarchical structure of this bilevel problem.
The algorithm employs two upper bounding techniques: a knapsack-scheduling relaxation that optimally solves a continuous allocation problem, and a Max-LPT approach that provides a $\frac{3}{4}$-approximation guarantee by combining longest processing time scheduling with knapsack optimization.
Computational experiments compare both bounding approaches against complete enumeration and against the MIBS solver from the literature.

\textbf{Keywords:} Bilevel optimization, branch-and-bound, knapsack problem, scheduling, combinatorial optimization
